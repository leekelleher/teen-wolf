THE STATE OF DECOUPLING UMBRACO IN 2019

Today, we have a variety of options for architecting our Umbraco solutions, (in terms of this talk, these would be typically Coupled to Decoupled).

How do you decide which is the best for your next Umbraco project?

Whether that would be a regular website, multi-tenancy website, mobile app, chatbots, kiosks, voice skills, or a combination of any of those.

We'll be exploring the pros & cons for each of these approaches,
to help you make an informed decision on which to choose, based on your project's requirements, developer skill-set, editorial needs and future-proofing.

What are our architecture options?

The common approaches are: Coupled and Decoupled… with everything in between.

What is Coupled?

Coupled would be the traditional single-server approach for housing both website front-end and CMS back-end.

What is Decoupled?

Whereas decoupled would be to separate concerns by detaching the front-end from the back-end, a la headless.

Decoupled could be thought of as a single category, that reflects a full separation of concerns between the presentation layer and all other aspects of the CMS.

The CMS would become a content repository (data provider), with (typically) a server-side application, responsible for rendering all the markup, and communicating with Umbraco via web service APIs, and pushing out to further channels.

The assumption here is that the front-end = web and back-end = CMS.

What is Decoupled?

When now... Front-end can be web, mobile apps, AMP, screen readers, XBox, Smart TVs, Alexa

All of these front-ends should be 1st Class Citizens ... moving away from the mindset that the Website will power other channels.

Breaking it down

HYBRID, PROGRESSIVELY DECOUPLED

FULLY DECOUPLED STATIC

FULLY DECOUPLED HEADLESS

The area of “Decoupled” can be broken down into further options...

Hybrid, Progressively Decoupled Umbraco
Fully decoupled static site (e.g. has presentation layer)
Fully decoupled app (e.g. 100% headless, zero concept of presentation layer)

These are a spectrum, on one side you have hybrid (left) with headless on the opposite end, we’ll go into detail in a moment.

These approaches depend on varying preferences and requirements.

What does this mean in terms of Umbraco?

Coupled

Traditional Umbraco

Coupled. This is Traditional Umbraco as is. CMS and website all in one place, rendering all the markup.

All of Umbraco's usual responsibilities stay intact. Umbraco as a monolithic system, remains in complete control over the presentation and content repository.

Hybrid

Progressively Decoupled Umbraco

Progressively Decoupled. From an infrastructure perspective, this is the same as Coupled, single-server running CMS and frontend.

With a slight difference… Umbraco is used for rendering minimal markup, with a JavaScript “framework” layered on top to populate content, (e.g. using APIs to query the CMS).

Why do this? Research has been done into load-times and the performance perception of user-experiences for interactive websites.

The technique is to use JavaScript for rendering parts of a page (anything from a single component, to the entire body content), in order to achieve faster (perceived) load times and responses.

An example of this, Facebook have an approach called BigPipe to pipeline parts of their webpages asynchronously.

Facebook BigPipe

An example of this, Facebook have an approach called BigPipe to pipeline parts of their webpages asynchronously.

Full Decoupled
Static Sites

Fully decoupled static site.

Use Umbraco as a content repository for a static site generator,

Static sites can offer improved performance, security, and reduced complexity for developers.

Static site generators like Gatsby would retrieve content from Umbraco, generate a static website, and deploy that static site to a CDN, usually through a specialized cloud provider such as Netlify.

The approach is gaining traction as a concept called the JAMstack!

JAMstack?

JAMstack is an emerging tech stack, it represents...

JavaScript, APIs, Markup
Individually, these aren’t nothing new, but together this new concept enables fully decoupled static sites.

We're going to hear a lot more about JAMstack during the next 12 months.

Full Decoupled
Headless

Fully decoupled app.

Use Umbraco as a content repository to be accessed by channels and other consumers.

In marketing, this is referred to by other names: Web Service APIs, content-as-a-service ... Umbraco will offer their Umbraco Headless product soon.

Umbraco has no knowledge of presentation layer, it's a data provider.

What's the different
in decoupling?

For the purposes of this talk,

Decoupled would a content push, out from the CMS

Whereas Headless would pull content from the CMS when requested

A push vs a pull.

What are the advantages
to each approach?

Advantages of Coupled

  • Established. What we already know.
  • All Umbraco features out-of-the-box
  • Previewing, URL management, etc
  • Content tree/structure drives website
Traditional Umbraco remains an excellent choice for editors who need full control over the visual elements on the page, with access to features such as layout (Grid), templating (if brave), previewing and URL management.

This is Umbraco as we have known it all along. The benefits are real, has value for clients.

Advantages of Hybrid

  • Same as Coupled
  • Performance gains
  • Steps towards Decoupling (mindset)
This is still how most new content management projects are built.

Advantages of Decoupled

(in general)

  • Clean, accessible and scaleable APIs
  • Separation of upgrades (from CMS)
  • Separation of channel redesigns
  • Less reliance on Umbraco specialists
  • Adopt cutting-edge frontend technologies
Clean APIs:
Publish to many frontends and experiences simultaneously.
Integrate multiple backends or services (a la Microservices)

Advantages of
Decoupled Static

  • Static, secure, CDN
  • Reproducible, repeatable
  • Technology choices
  • Cheap hosting
If your static site is compromised, then it can be nuked and re-deployed.

Technology choices:
in Ruby land, choose Jekyll
in JS land, chooose Gatsby, or MetalSmith
in .NET land, we have a generator called Wyam.

Advantages of Headless

  • True separation of concerns
  • Multi-channel
  • Future proof
Content comes first ... modern CMSs tend to manage websites first, and content second.

Future-proof: We don't know what new tech will emerge, but we can assume that it'll use our APIs.

What are the disadvantages
to each approach?

Disadvantages of Coupled

  • Hosting, memory, costs
  • Developer skill-set
  • Razor templating only
.NET hosting generally costs more. With cloud, say Azure, high memory usage is heavily frowned upon.
Generally a small-ish Umbraco site would run around 500Mb of memory. Which would increase with more content.

Disadvantages of Hybrid

  • Same as Coupled
  • Team size and skill-sets (both .NET and JS)
In terms of team skills, with Progressively Decoupled, you may need a dedicated JavaScript developer.
So this would increase your team size, (and costs)

From an Editors perspective, the less of the page dedicated to JavaScript, the more they can control the page through Umbraco Grid (or Stacked Content) capabilities.

Disadvantages of Decoupled

(in general)

  • Monolithic becomes Polylithic
  • Umbraco out-of-the-box functionality only works for the back-end
  • The minimum team size is higher for efficient development
  • Needs organisational buy-in
Monolithic: From a DevOps perspective, a single system is now a web of systems. More to manage and maintain.

Team: Same as Hybrid, you need diverse skills in your team.

Organisation: Your customer/organisation needs to have buy-in from the start, otherwise it can lead to confusion in the maintenance phase, when their mindset was with Traditional. They'd expect the one-person "webmaster" support.

Disadvantages of Decoupled Static

  • Lose built-in previewing
  • Lose built-in templating
  • Potential disconnect from Content structure
  • Dynamic features, e.g. forms/membership
If accustom to Umbraco's built-in features, then losing them is a major disadvantage.

Dynamc features, such as form submissions, membership login, and search functionality require further development.

Disadvantages of Headless

  • Same as Decoupled
  • Further disconnect from Editors
Disconnect: In terms of rich content, a non-CMS dev would prefer a flat "poor" model, e.g. key/value pairs.

Evaluation process

Are there things the
project cannot live without?

  • Editorial needs
  • Developer needs
Are there things the project cannot live without?

When deciding the architecture of an Umbraco website, you must assess the project requirements, from the perspective of both the Editor needs and the Developer needs.

This is a critical step in your evaluation process to weigh the different advantages and disadvantages. Keeping in mind your customers/organization needs.

It's important to remember that when editorial and marketing teams select a CMS, it's usually because of it's layout capabilities and rich editing functionality. Umbraco can offer editors the ability to control the layout in the browser, in terms of the Grid or Macros or the RTE.

Bringing this to Decoupled would require further development effort.

What can we live without?

Both editorial and developer needs are things that projects cannot live without,
and here are the questions you need to ask about your project:

What are the
Editors needs?

  • Content Layout - Grid, RTE, Macros, Stacked Content
  • Previews
  • Templating, Macros
  • Content tree, URL structure/management
  • Accessibility (WAI-ARIA) of content
I've mentioned Umbraco's built-in features a few times now, but again, features such as templating, previewing, macros, content layout (Grid), domains, redirects / URL management, are all essential Editorial needs.

Accessibility: This could be as simple as controling the ALT tags for an image.

What are the
Developers needs?

  • Control over visual presentation (instead of Editors)
  • Templating skill-set
  • Server-side rendering
  • Build Tasks (bundling & minification)
  • Data from APIs (GraphQL)
  • Security practices for those APIs
There could be a battle over control with the layout, maybe the Developer needs more control than the Editor?

Let’s look at
our options...

Considering all these questions about things your project cannot live without, let’s look at the options for whether your requirements reflect the needs of the Editor or the Developer, or a mix of both.

Option 1

Requirements reflect purely editorial needs

  • Will there be any user interactions?
  • Access multiple data sources?
This leads to 2 further questions...

If there are parts of the website that require user interactions with JavaScript?
This could be form submissions, member login, blog comments or even 'like' buttons.

Will the website need to pull in content from multiple data source via an API?
This could be something like pulling in user-data from a CRM.

If Yes...

(to either question)

Progressively Decoupled

If No...

(to either question)

Coupled
Traditional Umbraco

Option 2

Requirements reflect purely developer needs

  • Is it a static website or dynamic web application?
Static or Dynamic?

By Dynamic I mean, interactions, such as forms, membership, search, etc.

If Static...

Fully Decoupled
Static Site

If Dynamic...

Fully Decoupled
Headless

Option 3

Requirements reflect both editorial and developer needs

Progressively Decoupled

If your answers show that your requirements reflect a mix of both editorial and developer needs,
you should consider a Progressively Decoupled implementation, and your decision is complete.

Considerations

(if choosing any decoupled option)

Frontend developers may not have Umbraco/CMS experience or knowledge

This can heavily influence the experience of the CMS. e.g. “Designed by a Developer”

e.g. Think of the days before Nested Content, the CMS would have "Title 1", "Copy Text 1", "Title 2", "Copy Text 2", and so forth...

Who influences the CMS design/experience? Who dictates the richness of content?

Is it the client, or content-editor, or developer?

Again, this becomes about control and who is willing to give up control to other the side? Developers had control as "webmasters", then CMSes have pushed control to content-editors, now developers are looking to take control back.

Frontend developers would
opt for a simple 'model'

e.g. key/value data response

If you don't care about the Editor's experience, or are going to lose the richness of content, then why use Umbraco at all? Might as well be using WordPress.

Important message

Don't forget the Editor experience

When choosing decoupled, please don’t forget the Editor experience!

Current and future trends

for Editor & Developer complexities

We're seeing JavaScript frameworks become more complex, while static site generators have become less complex.

Only a couple of years ago, we'd have seen a modern web developer boast about how they used React for their personal blog. Which ended up with 3 blog posts and an About page. Today, this would have more likely been done using a static site generator.

Other considerations could be around workflows, in terms of build tasks and continuous integration.

We have choices

don't feel pressured

Key takeaway: We have choices, don't feel pressured into the latest trend. It’s OK to choose traditional Umbraco, with Progressively Decoupled.

In the next few years, I'm sure we'll see further innovations with Decoupled architectures.
We may even see new products emerge that will solve many of the disadvantages (with each approach) that I've outlined today.
